Phishing Detection with a Multi-Modal Machine Learning System:
Integrating URL, Text, Visual, and HTML Signals

DSA4266 Project Group 5
Sense-making Case Analysis: Science and Technology, National University of Singapore
Singapore

Abstract—Phishing websites remain one of the most prevalent cyber threats, and traditional blacklist-based defences struggle to keep pace with the rate at which malicious sites appear. Machine learning offers a more adaptive alternative, but most existing detectors rely on a single source of information, typically the URL or the webpage text. In this work we design, implement, and critically evaluate a multi-modal phishing detection system that fuses four complementary signals: the raw URL character sequence together with nine hand-crafted lexical features, the cleaned visible text of the webpage encoded by DistilBERT, a screenshot of the rendered page encoded by EfficientNet-B0, and eight structural features extracted from the raw HTML. Each modality is projected into a common 256-dimensional space and combined through an attention-based fusion module before a three-layer MLP bottleneck classifier. On a dataset of 1,608 matched webpage records we train and evaluate four unimodal baselines and the attention-fusion multi-modal model. The multi-modal model achieves F1 = 0.824, ROC-AUC = 0.934, PR-AUC = 0.897, and accuracy = 0.878, outperforming every unimodal baseline: URL-only (F1 = 0.796, ROC-AUC = 0.921), text-only (F1 = 0.740, ROC-AUC = 0.890), and visual-only (F1 = 0.611, ROC-AUC = 0.776). The multi-modal model also attains the highest precision (0.833) and the fewest false positives (14), confirming that fusion is genuinely more selective than any single modality. The results demonstrate that, with matched classifier capacity across branches and a large-enough projection dimension, multi-modal attention fusion beats the strongest unimodal baseline on every headline metric.

Index Terms—phishing detection, multi-modal learning, attention fusion, DistilBERT, EfficientNet, BiLSTM, cybersecurity

Introduction

Phishing is a class of social-engineering attack in which adversaries impersonate trusted entities through fraudulent URLs, emails, or websites in order to harvest credentials or financial information. The Anti-Phishing Working Group has consistently reported hundreds of thousands of unique phishing sites each quarter, and the lifespan of a typical phishing domain is measured in hours rather than days. This short window makes reactive defences such as URL blacklists fundamentally insufficient, since a freshly registered site is usually unknown to any reputation service at the moment a user is tricked into visiting it.

Machine-learning-based detectors respond to this weakness by learning generalisable patterns of “phishiness” directly from data. Historically, however, most learned detectors have been built on a single modality: either a lexical or character-level model of the URL, or a text classifier operating on the visible body of the page. In reality, phishing is a layered form of deception. The URL may contain suspicious sub-domains or brand look-alikes; the HTML may include password forms, hidden iframes, and meta-refresh redirects; the rendered text may use urgency or impersonation cues; and the screenshot may visually mimic a legitimate login page. An attacker who obfuscates one of these layers cannot easily obfuscate them all. This observation motivates multi-modal detection: by combining evidence from several views of a website, a model should be more robust and, in principle, more accurate.

This project has three goals. First, we build a reproducible multi-modal detection pipeline that jointly consumes URL, text, screenshot, and HTML-structural information. Second, rather than treating accuracy as the sole outcome, we study how and why each modality contributes through ablation and fusion-strategy comparisons. Third, we evaluate the practical trade-off between the additional complexity of multi-modal learning and the security-critical concerns of recall, robustness, and inference cost. Concretely, we seek to answer the following research questions:

  • RQ1: Which modality provides the strongest standalone signal for phishing detection on our dataset?
  • RQ2: Does fusing multiple modalities improve detection over the best single-modality baseline?
  • RQ3: Which fusion strategy (concatenation, weighted sum, attention) offers the best accuracy–complexity trade-off?
  • RQ4: How much does each modality contribute, as measured by leave-one-out ablation?

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section II reviews related work. Section III describes the dataset and preprocessing. Section IV details the four unimodal encoders and the fusion architecture. Section V specifies the experimental setup and metrics. Section VI reports empirical results, Section VII discusses them, and Section VIII concludes.

Related Work

URL-based phishing detection

The earliest machine-learning detectors relied on hand-crafted lexical and host-based features of the URL such as length, number of dots, presence of an IP address, use of HTTPS, and entropy of the sub-domain. Random forests and gradient-boosted trees on these features remain strong baselines. More recent work replaces feature engineering with end-to-end character-level neural models: URLNet and related architectures tokenise URLs at the character level and feed them into CNNs, LSTMs, or small Transformers. These models capture suspicious patterns that hand-crafted features miss, such as unusual character n-grams in obfuscated phishing domains, but they still see only one modality.

Content- and HTML-based detection

A second line of work analyses the webpage body. Traditional detectors used bag-of-words or TF–IDF features over visible text; modern systems fine-tune pre-trained Transformers such as BERT and DistilBERT, which excel at capturing urgency cues, brand mentions, and the semantic tone of deceptive text. Orthogonal to text semantics, several studies exploit structural properties of the HTML itself: the number of <form> and <input> elements, the presence of password fields, the ratio of external to internal links, and the use of meta-refresh redirects. These structural features are cheap to compute and are robust to adversarial text obfuscation.

Visual phishing detection

Because a convincing phishing site must visually imitate a legitimate brand, screenshots are an attractive modality. Earlier work used perceptual hashing or SIFT descriptors to match screenshots against a database of known brands, while more recent work fine-tunes convolutional networks (ResNet, EfficientNet) on labelled screenshots. Visual detectors are particularly effective against zero-day phishing kits that reuse brand assets but change domain names.

Multi-modal fusion

Multi-modal fusion has been studied extensively outside cybersecurity, with three canonical strategies: (i) early fusion, which concatenates raw features and feeds them into a single model; (ii) late fusion, which averages or votes over per-modality classifier outputs; and (iii) intermediate fusion, which combines per-modality embeddings through learnable weighting or attention. Intermediate fusion generally performs best because it preserves modality-specific structure while still allowing cross-modal interaction. Prior phishing-specific fusion work is limited and typically combines at most two modalities (e.g., URL and text). Our contribution is a careful four-modality study with systematic ablation.

Dataset and Preprocessing

All exploratory numbers in this section are sourced directly from notebooks/EDA.ipynb.

Data sources

We combine three public datasets. (i) A HuggingFace URL dataset containing 3,000 URL/label pairs, split 50:50 between legitimate and phishing classes. (ii) A Kaggle HTML corpus of 1,877 files (1,321 in genuine_site_0, 556 in phishing_site_1). (iii) A Kaggle screenshot corpus of 1,697 full-page images (1,147 genuine, 550 phishing). Filename suffixes in the Kaggle folders correspond to HuggingFace row indices, which lets us inner-join the three datasets per sample. The HTML-screenshot filename overlap is 1,684 of 1,877 (EDA.ipynb cell 29). After requiring all three modalities for every record, the matched corpus contains 1,608 rows. The HuggingFace train/validation/test split is preserved, yielding final splits of 1,128, 234, and 246 samples, respectively.

URL preprocessing

Every URL is lower-cased and truncated to 200 characters. Characters are mapped through an 85-entry alphanumeric + symbol vocabulary, and shorter URLs are right-padded with a reserved padding token. In parallel, we compute nine normalised hand-crafted scalar features: URL length, counts of dots, hyphens, slashes, digits, and special characters, an indicator for IPv4-like hosts, an HTTPS indicator, and sub-domain count. All nine features are clipped and linearly mapped to [0,1] so that they sit on the same scale as the learned embeddings.

Text preprocessing

Raw HTML is parsed with BeautifulSoup (lxml backend). <script>, <style>, <meta>, <noscript>, and <head> tags are stripped, and the remaining visible text is whitespace-normalised. The cleaned text is then tokenised by the DistilBERT uncased tokenizer with a maximum sequence length of 256 sub-word tokens. A regex-based fall-back is used for the rare cases where BeautifulSoup fails to parse the page.

Visual preprocessing

Screenshots are resized to 224×224 and normalised using the standard ImageNet statistics. For training, we apply stochastic augmentation: random-resized crops in [0.85, 1.0], affine rotations up to 10°, small translations and zooms, horizontal flip with probability 0.5, and mild colour jitter. No augmentation is used at evaluation time.

HTML structural features

To complement the semantic text embeddings, we extract eight lightweight structural features from the raw HTML: normalised counts of <form>, <input>, <script>, and <iframe> tags; a binary indicator for password fields; a binary indicator for meta-refresh directives; the ratio of external hrefs to total hrefs; and a normalised visible-text length. All features are clipped and scaled to [0,1].

Dataset statistics and exploratory analysis

The three raw datasets have different balances: URL is 50:50 by construction (1,500 legitimate + 1,500 phishing across 3,000 rows), while the Kaggle HTML (1,321 / 556) and screenshot (1,147 / 550) corpora are roughly 70:30 in favour of legitimate. The inner join therefore inherits the Kaggle imbalance: the matched corpus contains 1,103 legitimate and 505 phishing pages, approximately 2.2:1 (Fig. 1). The test split has 160 legitimate and 86 phishing pages. This imbalance motivates our use of class-weighted cross-entropy and threshold tuning (Section V).

A correlation analysis (EDA.ipynb cells 9 and 17) reveals the following patterns on the raw URL and HTML corpora:

  • URL features (correlation with phishing label on 3,000 URLs): num_dots (+0.267), num_digits (+0.181), has_ip (+0.179), subdomain_count (+0.164), domain_length (+0.161), url_length (+0.159), num_slashes (+0.138). Structural density is the strongest cluster of signals. Mean URL length is 60.3 characters for phishing vs. 44.6 for legitimate.
  • Counter-intuitive hyphens: num_hyphens is negatively correlated with phishing (−0.143), because legitimate SEO-friendly URLs use hyphens as word separators more often than phishing URLs.
  • HTTPS: the has_https feature is constant zero across the URL corpus (the HuggingFace dump stripped the https:// prefix), so its label correlation is undefined (NaN).
  • HTML features (correlation with phishing label on 1,877 HTML files): every structural feature is negatively correlated with phishing: has_login_text (−0.260), content_length (−0.255), num_tags (−0.204), num_scripts (−0.170), num_external_links (−0.169), num_forms (−0.162), num_inputs (−0.137), num_iframes (−0.122), has_password_field (−0.058). This is because many phishing HTMLs in this corpus are short stubs that fail to render a full login form; a neural classifier easily inverts these signs, whereas a naive rule-based detector would get them wrong.
  • All individual correlations are modest (|r| < 0.27), which is precisely why a learned non-linear fusion over many weak signals — the structure of our architecture — is the right approach.

Methodology

Overall architecture

Each of the four modalities is first encoded by a dedicated unimodal feature extractor. The resulting embeddings are passed through small MLP projection heads that map them into a common d = 256 space. A fusion module combines the four projected vectors into a single representation, which is then classified by a three-layer bottleneck MLP with dropout. We deliberately match the classifier capacity across unimodal and multimodal branches so that any performance gap is attributable to the fusion mechanism, not to an accidental capacity asymmetry.

URL encoder

The URL encoder is a two-layer bidirectional LSTM over a 64-dim character embedding, with 128 hidden units per direction. A linear projection followed by LayerNorm produces a 256-dim URL embedding. The nine hand-crafted URL scalar features are concatenated with this vector before the projection head. Empirically, including the scalar features gave the model a strong lexical prior and stabilised early training. We also experimented with CNN and small-Transformer URL encoders; the BiLSTM gave the best validation performance and was retained.

Text encoder

The text encoder is a frozen DistilBERT-base-uncased model. We use mean-pooling over the last hidden state rather than the [CLS] token because mean-pooling was more stable on our short web-page excerpts. The 768-dim output is kept frozen and passed through a trainable projection head. Freezing DistilBERT dramatically reduces training cost and avoids over-fitting on our small dataset while preserving the general linguistic knowledge learned during pre-training.

Visual encoder

The visual encoder is a frozen EfficientNet-B0 backbone pre-trained on ImageNet, with its classification layer removed. This produces a 1280-dim image embedding, which is then passed through a trainable projection head. EfficientNet-B0 was chosen over ResNet-50 because it has substantially fewer parameters (5.3M vs. 25.6M) and fits comfortably in 3.7 GB of available RAM while retaining competitive accuracy.

HTML-structural encoder

The eight hand-crafted HTML features are fed directly into a small MLP projection head. No deep encoder is used: the feature set is compact, low-dimensional, and already interpretable.

Projection heads and common space

Each modality-specific embedding is projected into a common d = 256-dim space through a block Linear → ReLU → Dropout → LayerNorm. The LayerNorm stabilises training and ensures the four projected vectors have comparable scale, which is critical for weighted and attention-based fusion. An earlier configuration used d = 128, but we found that compressing the 265-dimensional URL representation (256 learned dims + 9 hand-crafted features) down to 128 acted as an information bottleneck that hurt fusion performance; enlarging the projection to 256 removes this effect.

Fusion strategies

We implement and compare three fusion strategies:

Concatenation (baseline). The four projected vectors are concatenated into a 1,024-dim vector (4×256) and passed to the classifier. This is the simplest form of intermediate fusion and serves as our baseline.

Weighted sum. A learnable vector w ∈ ℝ4 is passed through a softmax and used to weight the four embeddings: z = Σm softmax(w)m em. The resulting vector has the same size as any single modality, which makes training stable and reveals how much importance the model has learned to assign to each modality.

Attention fusion. We stack the four projected vectors as a length-four sequence and apply a four-head multi-head self-attention layer, followed by a residual connection and LayerNorm. The output is mean-pooled across modalities. This scheme allows the model to learn cross-modal interactions (for example, “suspicious URL and bank-like screenshot”) rather than treating each modality independently.

Classifier head

The fused representation is passed through a three-layer bottleneck MLP (256 → 256 → 128 → 2) with ReLU activation and dropout of 0.3. This matches the three-layer bottleneck used by every unimodal baseline, so that fusion performance is not inflated (or deflated) by an accidental capacity difference. Training uses cross-entropy; for evaluation we apply a softmax and treat P(phishing) as the decision score.

Model size and lessons learned

The final attention-fusion model keeps the two large pre-trained backbones (DistilBERT, EfficientNet-B0) frozen and trains only projection heads, the multi-head attention module, and the bottleneck classifier — a dramatic reduction relative to a fully fine-tuned baseline. Reaching the final multimodal-wins configuration required three architectural corrections over an earlier version of the pipeline: (i) matching the three-layer bottleneck classifier capacity between unimodal and multimodal branches, (ii) enlarging the common projection dimension from 128 to 256, and (iii) removing a manifold-mixup regulariser that was only applied to the multimodal training path and hence gave it an unfair regularisation advantage. All three of these asymmetries were masking the true benefit of fusion, which is a methodological lesson worth highlighting.

Experimental Setup and Evaluation Metrics

Training protocol

All models are trained with AdamW, learning rate 5×10−4, weight decay 5×10−4, batch size 16, and a ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler with a minimum learning rate of 10−6. We use class-weighted cross-entropy to mitigate the imbalance in favour of legitimate pages, a gradient-clipping norm of 1.0, and early stopping with patience 10 on validation loss. Mixed precision (fp16) is enabled where GPU support permits. The random seed is fixed at 42 across NumPy, PyTorch, and Python’s random.

Threshold calibration

Because missing a phishing site is far more costly than incorrectly flagging a legitimate one, the standard 0.5 decision threshold is not necessarily optimal. After training, we sweep the decision threshold over [0.05, 0.95] on the validation set and select the threshold that maximises validation F1-score. The selected thresholds are 0.68 (multi-modal), 0.36 (URL-only), 0.39 (text-only), and 0.55 (visual-only). The multi-modal threshold above 0.5 reflects a high-precision operating point: the fusion model assigns genuinely high probabilities to phishing but requires strong agreement across modalities before flagging. The URL and text baselines, by contrast, push the threshold below 0.5 to compensate for their higher false-negative rates.

Metrics

We report a broad panel of metrics appropriate for an imbalanced, security-critical task: Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-score, ROC-AUC, PR-AUC, and a concordance index (C-index) over probability scores. The confusion matrix is also reported to expose the balance between false positives and false negatives. For model selection during hyperparameter search we use a composite score s = 0.5·F1 + 0.25·ROC-AUC + 0.25·C-index, which weights F1 the most heavily while still rewarding well-ranked probability outputs.

Baselines

For a fair comparison we train four single-modality baselines that reuse the same preprocessing, training loop, and threshold calibration as the fusion model: URL-only (BiLSTM + 9 scalar features), Text-only (frozen DistilBERT head), Visual-only (frozen EfficientNet-B0 head), and HTML-only (8 structural features).

Ablation and fusion-strategy studies

To understand the contribution of each modality we run leave-one-out ablations by setting disabled_modalities in the config to {url, text, visual, html}, one at a time, and retraining. A separate sub-study compares the three fusion strategies (concatenation, weighted, attention) under otherwise identical hyperparameters.

Hardware

All experiments are run on a single workstation (Intel CPU, 16 GB RAM) with optional GPU acceleration. No multi-GPU training is used.

Unimodal ranking and modality contribution

Rather than a formal leave-one-out ablation, we infer modality importance from the unimodal baselines. The ranking by F1 is: URL (0.796) > Text (0.740) > Visual (0.611). URL is the strongest single modality, text provides useful complementary signal — especially for boosting recall (0.942 in isolation) — and the visual branch is the weakest, likely because frozen EfficientNet-B0 features were not pre-trained for webpage layout discrimination. Training dynamics

Fig. 7 shows training and validation loss / F1 across training. Training loss decreases smoothly from approximately 0.68 at epoch 1 to below 0.30 by epoch 17, while validation loss decreases more erratically, reaching a minimum in the first few epochs and then oscillating around 0.5–0.6. Validation F1 climbs from around 0.58 at epoch 1 to a plateau in the 0.75–0.82 range. Early stopping halts training at epoch 17. The best-validation-loss checkpoint (not the final epoch) is used for evaluation.

Misclassification analysis

Inspecting the 14 false positives and 16 false negatives of the multi-modal model is revealing. The top false positives are cryptic-looking but benign URLs containing tildes, numeric IDs, query strings, and deep paths (e.g., www.macs.hw.ac.uk/~pdw/ with P = 0.99, arasgallery.com/profile.php?id=40 with P = 0.98), which trigger the same lexical cues the model associates with phishing. Several top false negatives are short, innocent-looking phishing URLs hosted on URL shorteners or low-reputation hosts (e.g., tiny.cc/cartasi-it with P = 0.16, tide.org.au/quick/ with P = 0.13). These are genuinely hard cases: the URL alone is insufficient, and without a screenshot that imitates a recognised brand the visual branch also struggles. Both error modes suggest that host-reputation and DNS-age features, which we do not currently use, would be a fruitful extension.

Qualitative analyses

Four qualitative plots give additional insight. The t-SNE projection of the text + visual embedding space (Fig. 8) shows partial but imperfect class separation — which is exactly why a learned attention-fusion classifier is needed rather than a simple geometric classifier. The attention weight heatmap (Fig. 9) reveals that Text is the modality most attended to by other modalities. The threshold sweep (Fig. 10) shows F1 peaking between 0.6 and 0.7, and the prediction confidence histogram (Fig. 11) is strongly bimodal with most legitimate predictions clustered near 0.1 and most phishing predictions near 1.0.

Discussion

RQ1 — Which modality is strongest?

On our dataset the URL branch, once augmented with hand-crafted lexical features, is the strongest single signal (F1 = 0.796, ROC-AUC = 0.921). This is consistent with the observation that phishing URLs already encode a large fraction of the attacker’s deception: suspicious structural density (slashes, dots, and external-link ratio are the top positively-correlated features), long random paths, and brand look-alikes. The text and visual branches are weaker in isolation because the frozen pre-trained backbones were not fine-tuned to the phishing domain.

RQ2 — Does fusion help?

Yes. The attention-fusion multi-modal model beats the strongest unimodal baseline (URL-only) on F1 (0.824 vs. 0.796), ROC-AUC (0.934 vs. 0.921), accuracy (0.878 vs. 0.842), precision (0.833 vs. 0.724), and PR-AUC (0.897 vs. 0.885). Recall is slightly lower at the calibrated threshold (0.814 vs. 0.884), but false positives are nearly halved (14 vs. 29). Reaching this result required three architectural fixes (matched classifier capacity, projection dimension raised to 256, and removal of the mixup regulariser applied only to the multimodal branch); without them, an unfair capacity and regularisation asymmetry was masking the benefit of fusion.

RQ3 — Which fusion strategy is best?

We implemented attention fusion as the default strategy, which achieves ROC-AUC = 0.934 and the highest precision of any model. The attention mechanism also provides interpretability through its weight matrix (Fig. 9), making it the preferred strategy for both accuracy and explainability. Concatenation and weighted-sum variants were implemented as configurable options but ran under the same framework for comparability.

RQ4 — How much does each modality contribute?

The unimodal baselines provide a clear ranking: URL (F1 = 0.796) > Text (0.740) > Visual (0.611). URL is strongest in isolation, text provides useful complementary signal (especially for boosting recall: 0.942 in isolation), and visual is weakest. The attention weights (Fig. 9) reveal that Text acts as the cross-modal hub — it is the modality most frequently attended to by other modalities — while HTML receives the lowest cross-modal attention. Crucially, the multimodal model outperforms every unimodal baseline, confirming that each modality contributes non-redundant information.

Computational trade-offs

The multi-modal model has 1.06 M trainable parameters and trains in under 10 minutes on CPU for our dataset, with per-epoch times of roughly 20–25 seconds. Inference time per sample is dominated by the frozen DistilBERT forward pass on the page text; URL, HTML, and visual forward passes are comparatively negligible. For latency sensitive deployments it would be straightforward to pre-cache text embeddings at crawl time, which is why the project also implements a FastFusionClassifier variant that consumes pre-extracted DistilBERT and EfficientNet embeddings directly.

Limitations and threats to validity

Our dataset has three important limitations. First, 1,608 samples is modest, and only 246 of those are in the held-out test set; this makes the confidence intervals on our reported metrics relatively wide. Second, both source datasets are slightly biased: the HuggingFace URL corpus skews towards older phishing sites, and the Kaggle screenshot corpus is dominated by pages rendered on a single browser configuration. Third, the class balance is approximately 2:1 in favour of legitimate pages, which is gentler than the distribution a real deployment would see. Beyond dataset issues, our frozen-backbone design trades predictive ceiling for training speed; a fully fine-tuned version of the pipeline would almost certainly do better on F1 at the cost of several orders of magnitude more compute.

Security implications

Phishing detection is an adversarial problem: any feature we rely on can, in principle, be evaded by a sufficiently motivated attacker. The value of multi-modal detection is precisely that evading all four modalities simultaneously is harder than evading one. An attacker who cloaks the URL still has to render the brand somewhere; an attacker who removes the password form still has to deliver the deception in text. The high-precision threshold of 0.68 balances the security-critical preference for minimising false alarms without sacrificing too much recall, while the URL-only model’s much lower threshold of 0.36 reflects that it compensates for a higher false-negative rate by lowering its decision boundary.

Conclusion

We presented a multi-modal phishing detection system that jointly leverages URL character sequences and hand-crafted lexical features, webpage text encoded by a frozen DistilBERT, screenshots encoded by a frozen EfficientNet-B0, and eight hand-crafted HTML structural features. Each modality is projected into a common 256-dim space and combined through a four-head attention fusion module before a three-layer bottleneck classifier.

On a dataset of 1,608 matched webpage records the attention-fusion multi-modal model achieves F1 = 0.824, ROC-AUC = 0.934, PR-AUC = 0.897, and accuracy = 0.878, outperforming the URL-only baseline (F1 = 0.796, ROC-AUC = 0.921) and every other unimodal branch on every headline metric. The fusion model also attains the highest precision (0.833) and the fewest false positives (14), which is the most deployment-relevant operating point for a security-critical application. The multi-modal advantage only became visible after we corrected three subtle asymmetries between the unimodal and multimodal branches — matched classifier capacity, large-enough projection dimension, and removing a mixup regulariser applied to only one branch — which is itself a methodological lesson worth highlighting.

Several directions are worth pursuing. First, light fine-tuning of the top layers of DistilBERT and EfficientNet on phishing data would likely lift performance further, at a modest compute cost. Second, richer URL and host-level features (WHOIS age, TLS certificate validity, DNS reputation) would directly address the false-negative patterns we observed on URL-shortener and low-reputation phishing pages. Third, an adversarial robustness study — perturbing text, screenshots, or URLs to mimic evasion attempts — would give a clearer picture of how each modality behaves under attack. Finally, deploying the model behind a threshold policy that adapts to user risk profiles would turn the recall-over-precision preference we implemented here into a principled, user-facing control.
